About the project

A visual shortcut to the ideas calculus is actually about.

Most calculus courses move fast: limits become derivatives, derivatives become rates, rates become areas and volumes — and somewhere in there, the intuition gets lost under notation. The Derivative Directory exists to put that intuition back on screen.

Every model is built in Desmos, refined for clarity, and organized by the same units you'll find in an AP Calculus course. Enter your own function, your own bounds, your own homework problem — and watch the concept respond in real time.

A note on performance: a few models — especially the 3D solids — are computationally heavy and may take a moment to render. Give them a few seconds, and avoid zooming in on any on-screen text inside the Desmos canvas.

How it works

From your lesson plan to the screen in four steps

Models are designed to sit beside your teaching — not replace it. Here's the typical path from "I need to explain this" to "now they get it."

  1. 01

    Pick your unit

    Head to the Model Library and filter by topic — Limits, Differentiation, Differential Equations, Related Rates, Integration, or Volume.

  2. 02

    Open the model

    Every card links straight to a live Desmos graph — in regular 2D or the 3D calculator. No account, no install, just a browser tab.

  3. 03

    Drop in your problem

    Type in the function, point, bounds, or differential equation from your lesson or homework. Most models come with a small built-in interface for exactly this.

  4. 04

    Explore and explain

    Drag points, run animations, and read off slopes, sums, and volumes as they update — live, on the board or on a student's own screen.

Who it's for

Two audiences, one set of models

Built to project at the front of the room. Use a model alongside traditional instruction to give visual learners something concrete to follow — then load your own textbook or exam-style question and work it live.

  • Review before a test, one unit at a time
  • Practice with built-in example problems

Twelve models, six calculus units

Every model opens directly in Desmos. Captions are tongue-in-cheek on purpose — the math underneath is not.

2D Limits & Continuity

Limit Calculator & Table

Graphs any function you enter, then builds a two-sided limit table around a chosen x-value — instantly showing whether the limit exists and what it equals.

Use it for: tedious limit problems, and for showing that a limit doesn't always equal the function's value at that point.

Continuity Proof Practice

A single worked function set up as a continuity example — ready for walking through the formal three-part continuity test.

Use it for: reviewing AP-style continuity proofs. Close the equations tab before presenting.

2D Differentiation

Secant-to-Tangent Animator

Draws a secant line through two points on a curve, then animates the second point sliding in until the secant collapses into the tangent line.

Use it for: visually proving that the derivative is the limit of the difference quotient.

2D Differentiation

Differentiability Proof Practice

Another single worked function, this time set up to test where a function fails to be differentiable — and why.

Use it for: the differentiability unit, reviewing AP-style justification of non-differentiable points.

2D Differentiation

Tangent Line Solver

Draws the tangent line at any point on your function, reports its slope, and writes out the line in both slope-intercept and point-slope form.

Use it for: any problem that asks for "the derivative at a point" or "the equation of the tangent line."

2D Differential Equations

Slope Field Generator

Enter any differential equation, set your bounds and step size, and get a full slope field — color-coded by steepness so direction is visible at a glance.

Use it for: skipping the tedious hand-drawing of slope fields while still seeing exactly how they're built.

Open in Desmos
2D Differential Equations

Euler's Method Stepper

Enter a differential equation and a step size (Δx), then iterate Euler's method point by point — with each linear approximation drawn as it's calculated.

Use it for: generating Euler's method values and seeing linearization happen rather than just computing it.

Open in Desmos
2D Differential Equations

Newton's Method Visualizer

Enter a function and drag a starting point on the x-axis, then iterate Newton's method to watch successive tangent lines walk toward a root.

Use it for: visualizing repeated linearization toward a zero — and getting the values without the by-hand arithmetic.

Open in Desmos
3D Related Rates

The Draining Cone

A fully animated 3D cone that drains at a constant rate of height or volume — your choice. Live meters track height, volume, and the related rate of change as the water level drops.

Use it for: the classic related-rates cone problem. Watching the water level fall alongside the meters makes the relationship between variables click.

Open in Desmos 3D
2D Integration

Riemann Sum Explorer

Enter a function and bounds, then switch between left, right, middle, and trapezoidal approximations — with the exact integral, each sum's value, and percent error shown side by side.

Use it for: seeing how a Riemann sum converges to a definite integral, and for checking sums that are painfully slow by hand.

Open in Desmos
3D Volume & Solids

Solids of Revolution Builder

Enter your functions, bounds, and axis of rotation to generate both the smooth, continuous solid and a blocky cross-sectional approximation — for revolution around either axis.

Use it for: volume problems where a region is revolved around an axis. An "active use" panel lets you toggle which parts of the model are visible.

Open in Desmos 3D
3D Volume & Solids

Cross-Section Solid Builder

Define a region by its bounding functions, then stack cross-sections — squares, equilateral or right triangles, and semicircles — to build the resulting solid in 3D.

Use it for: volume problems where a solid is formed by stacking known cross-sections on a base region. Same toggleable "active use" panel as the revolution builder.
