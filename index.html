About the project

A visual demonstration of the ideas formulas represent.

Math can be abstract: differential equations and solids of revolution are not easy to visualize. At some point, intuition is lost to notation and endless formulas. The Derivative Directory helps bring back true understanding.

Every model is built in Desmos and made as user-friendly as possible. Models are organized by math course and unit. Enter your own function or homework problem and watch the visual work.

A note on performance: a few models — especially the 3D solids — are computationally heavy and may take a moment to render. Give them a few seconds to load before troubleshooting. Additionally, avoid zooming in on any on-screen text inside the Desmos canvas.

How it works

From your lesson plan to the screen in four steps

Models are designed to assist your teaching, not replace it. Here's how to find and use the model that matches your needs.

  1. 01

    Pick your unit

    Head to the Model Library and filter by math course and unit.

  2. 02

    Open the model

    Every card links straight to a live Desmos graph, in 2D or 3D. No account or install, just a browser tab.

  3. 03

    Drop in your problem

    Type in all relevant information (function, points, bounds, etc.) from your problem. Most models come with a small built-in interface for this process.

  4. 04

    Explore and explain

    Drag points and run live animations and watch the model produce colorful visuals.

Model library

Twelve models

Every model opens directly in Desmos. Instructions are provided in the equations tab. Manipulate variables in the user tab or, if provided, use the UI in the graph itself.

2D Limits & Continuity

Limit Calculator & Table

Graphs any function you enter, then builds a two-sided limit table around a chosen x-value. Shows whether the limit exists and, if it does, what it equals.

Use it for: tedious limit problems, and for showing that a limit doesn't always equal the function's value at that point.

2D Limits & Continuity

Continuity Proof Practice

A simple, AP-style continuity practice problem. Contains the three different types of discontinuity.

Use it for: reviewing AP-style continuity proofs. Close the equations tab before presenting.

Open in Desmos
2D Differentiation

Difference Quotients

Draws a secant line through two points on a curve, then animates the second point sliding in until the secant collapses into the tangent line. Visually animates \(\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h} \)

Use it for: visually proving that the derivative is the limit of the difference quotient.

Open in Desmos
2D Differentiation

Differentiability Proof Practice

Another simple, AP-style practice problem, this time with the three different ways a function can fail to be differentiable.

Use it for: the differentiability unit, reviewing AP-style justification of non-differentiable points.

Open in Desmos
2D Differentiation

Tangent Line Calculator

Draws the tangent line at any point on your function and writes out the line in both slope-intercept and point-slope form.

Use it for: any problem that asks for "the derivative at a point" or "the equation of the tangent line."

Open in Desmos
2D Differential Equations

Slope Field Generator

Enter any differential equation with bounds and step size, and get a full slope field, color-coded by steepness.

Use it for: skipping the tedious hand-drawing of slope fields while still seeing exactly how they're built.

Open in Desmos
2D Differential Equations

Euler's Method

Enter a differential equation and a step size (Δx), then iterate Euler's method. Each linear approximation is drawn and slope is already calculated.

Use it for: generating Euler's method values and seeing linearization happen rather than just computing it.

Open in Desmos
2D Differential Equations

Newton's Method

Enter a function and drag a starting point, then iterate Newton's method to have tangent lines approach a zero. Note that normal restrictions (horizontal tangents, etc.) for Newton's method will apply.

Use it for: visualizing repeated linearization toward a zero.

Open in Desmos

3D Related Rates

The Cone Problem

A fully animated 3D cone that drains at a constant rate of height or volume. Height, volume, and the non-constant rate of change are displayed.

Use it for: the classic related-rates cone problem. Watching the rate of change fluctuate alongside seeing the cone offers a common-sense explanation for the complex math.

Open in Desmos 3D
2D Integration

Riemann Sums

Enter a function and bounds, then switch between left, right, middle, and trapezoidal approximations. The exact integral, each sum's value, and percent error are shown.

Use it for: seeing how a Riemann sum converges to a definite integral, and for checking sums that are painfully slow by hand.

Open in Desmos
2D Sequences and Series

Taylor Series

Enter a function, bounds, center, and the number of terms. The Taylor Series approximation of the function will appear. The portion of the series that is within a certain error margin (that you can change) will be highlighted.

Use it for: seeing how a Taylor Series approximation actually looks and how it becomes progressively more accurate.

Open in Desmos
3D Volume & Solids

Solids of Revolution

Enter your function(s), bounds, and the axis of rotation. Generates both the smooth, continuous solid and a blocky cross-sectional approximation to visually show how as the number of intervals approaches infinity, the shape becomes smooth.

Use it for: volume problems where a region is revolved around an axis. An "active use" folder lets you toggle which parts of the model are visible.

Open in Desmos 3D
3D Volume & Solids

Cross-Section Solids

Enter the functions and bounds, then choose the cross-section to stack (squares, semicircles, equilateral triangles, and right triangles) and see the resulting solid in 3D. Blocky cross-sections are also available.

Use it for: volume problems where a solid is formed by stacking known cross-sections on a base region. Same toggleable "active use" folder as the solids of revolution. Most parts of the active use folder have corresponding buttons on the graph itself.
